Respiratory & Pulmonary 7 Qs
Q1Respiratory

During an acute asthma attack, mast cells and eosinophils release cysteinyl leukotrienes, which bind to cysteinyl leukotriene receptors (G-protein coupled receptor), triggering intracellular signalling via phospholipase C → IP₃. Which ion is subsequently released from the endoplasmic reticulum, ultimately causing bronchoconstriction?

✦ Answer
Calcium (Ca²⁺) — IP₃ binds to IP₃ receptors on the endoplasmic reticulum, releasing Ca²⁺ into the cytoplasm → smooth muscle contraction → bronchoconstriction.

Q5Respiratory📊 Graph

During strenuous anaerobic exercise with lactic acidosis:
(a) Which direction does the oxygen–haemoglobin dissociation curve shift?
(b) What happens to Hb saturation?
(c) What happens to oxygen delivery (DO₂) to tissues?

✦ Answer
(a) Shift to the right (Bohr effect: ↑H⁺ + ↑CO₂ from lactic acid).
(b) Hb saturation decreases at any given PO₂.
(c) DO₂ increases — more O₂ is unloaded per unit blood to the working tissues.
Q6Respiratory

At high altitude, hypocapnia (from hyperventilation) raises CSF pH, initially inhibiting the central respiratory drive. Over the following days, what change occurs in CSF composition that restores central chemoreceptor sensitivity?

✦ Answer
Decreased CSF bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) — the choroid plexus actively transports HCO₃⁻ out of CSF to compensate for respiratory alkalosis, restoring CSF pH toward 7.32, allowing central chemoreceptors to re-activate the hypoxic drive.
Q7Respiratory

A patient using a supplemental oxygen mask: What happens to alveolar PO₂ (PAO₂)?

✦ Answer
Alveolar PO₂ increases. By the Alveolar Gas Equation: PAO₂ = FiO₂ × (Pb − PH₂O) − PaCO₂/R. Increasing FiO₂ directly raises PAO₂.

Cardiovascular 5 Qs
Q8Cardiovascular📊 Graph

The jugular venous pressure (JVP) waveform is shown below with components a, c, x, v, y labelled. At which point on the JVP waveform does the tricuspid valve open?

✦ Answer
Point y — the y descent represents opening of the tricuspid valve at the end of isovolumetric relaxation, allowing rapid passive ventricular filling. a = atrial contraction; c = tricuspid closure bump; x = atrial relaxation; v = venous filling during systole; y = tricuspid opens.
Q9Cardiovascular

Capsaicin activates TRPV1 receptors on cardiac afferent C-fibres, triggering a triad of bradycardia, hypotension, and apnoea. What is the name of this reflex?

✦ Answer
Bezold–Jarisch reflex — intense vagal (parasympathetic) activation via cardiac afferent C-fibre stimulation causes the classic triad: bradycardia, hypotension, and apnoea.
Q10Cardiovascular🩸 Haem

In the pressure–flow relationship of blood (driving pressure vs. flow graph), polycythaemia vera is present. At which pressure range does the curve become non-linear, and why? What is this effect called?

✦ Answer
Non-linearity occurs at low driving pressures. This is the Fahraeus–Lindqvist effect — in small vessels, RBC axial migration reduces apparent viscosity. Polycythaemia vera elevates true viscosity, disrupting linearity and causing the pressure-flow curve to deviate especially in the low-pressure region.
Q11Cardiovascular🩸 Haem

The Fahraeus–Lindqvist effect is most prominent at a certain vessel radius. Is blood viscosity increased or decreased compared to larger vessels at this radius?

✦ Answer
Blood viscosity decreases — in vessels <300 µm diameter, RBCs migrate axially, leaving a cell-depleted plasma layer near the wall, reducing apparent (effective) viscosity below that measured in large tubes.
Q65Cardiovascular📊 Graph

The Frank-Starling graph below plots stroke volume against left ventricular end-diastolic pressure (LVEDP) for three different states of myocardial contractility (A, B, C).
(a) Which curve represents normal cardiac contractility?
(b) Which curve represents a patient with congestive heart failure and severely reduced contractility?

✦ Answer
(a) Curve A — normal Frank-Starling relationship: increased preload (LVEDP) produces increased stroke volume up to a physiological maximum.
(b) Curve C — severe reduction in myocardial contractility (e.g., decompensated heart failure, dilated cardiomyopathy) shifts the curve downward and to the right, so stroke volume is markedly reduced at any given preload. Curve B represents moderate contractile impairment.

Renal & Fluid Balance 7 Qs
Q12Renal

(a) Which hormone increases H⁺ secretion and Na⁺ reabsorption in the renal tubule?
(b) Sympathetic activity stimulates renin secretion — what is the first messenger?
(c) What is the second messenger (intracellular signal) for renin secretion?

✦ Answer
(a) Aldosterone (mineralocorticoid) — acts on collecting duct principal cells.
(b) First messenger: Norepinephrine (NE) on β₁ adrenergic receptors of JG cells.
(c) Second messenger: cAMP → PKA → renin vesicle exocytosis.
Q13Neurosensory

In olfactory receptor neurons, odorant binding leads to cAMP production. What ion channel does cAMP directly bind to, causing Na⁺/Ca²⁺ influx and depolarisation?

✦ Answer
Cyclic Nucleotide-Gated (CNG) cation channel — directly opened by cAMP binding (no kinase needed), allowing Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ influx → depolarisation of the olfactory receptor neuron.
Q14Renal

Vasopressin (ADH) increases water reabsorption via aquaporin-2 (AQP2). In which tubular segment does ADH primarily exert this action?

✦ Answer
Collecting duct — ADH binds V₂ receptors on principal cells → Gs → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA → phosphorylation and apical insertion of AQP2.
Q15Renal⚗️ Endo

In end-stage kidney disease, the kidney fails to excrete a substance that accumulates in plasma. This elevated substance impairs renal calcium reabsorption, causing low serum calcium. What is this retained substance?

✦ Answer
Phosphate (inorganic phosphate) — phosphate retention in CKD suppresses 1α-hydroxylase (↓ calcitriol), impairs intestinal Ca²⁺ absorption, and forms calcium-phosphate complexes → secondary hyperparathyroidism.
Q16Renal

An elderly man with GI haemorrhage: BP 85/60 mmHg, HR 150 bpm, low serum osmolarity, elevated plasma ADH.
(a) What happens to ADH secretion from the posterior pituitary?
(b) What are the expected urine volume and urine osmolarity?

✦ Answer
(a) ADH secretion increases — haemorrhagic hypotension triggers non-osmotic (baroreceptor-mediated) ADH release that overrides the low osmolarity signal.
(b) Urine volume decreases; urine osmolarity increases (maximally concentrated urine, up to ~1200 mOsm/kg).
Q17Renal

Sympathetic nervous system activation causes vasoconstriction of renal afferent and efferent arterioles. Which neurotransmitter mediates this vasoconstriction?

✦ Answer
Norepinephrine (NE) — via α₁-adrenergic receptors on arteriolar smooth muscle → vasoconstriction → ↓ GFR.
Q18Renal⚗️ Endo

A type 1 diabetic in ketoacidosis receives IV insulin only.
(a) What is the kidney's response regarding fluid/electrolyte balance during compensation?
(b) What happens to plasma potassium after insulin treatment?

✦ Answer
(a) Kidney responds with increased volume reabsorption and acid excretion as pH corrects.
(b) Plasma K⁺ decreases (hypokalaemia) — insulin drives K⁺ into cells via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase stimulation; without K⁺ supplementation, life-threatening hypokalaemia results.
Q19Renal⚗️ Endo

Plasma inorganic phosphate is elevated.
(a) What happens to secretion of Fibroblast Growth Factor 23 (FGF-23)?
(b) What is the membrane-bound co-receptor required for FGF-23 signalling in the kidney?

✦ Answer
(a) FGF-23 secretion increases — osteocytes sense elevated phosphate and release FGF-23 to promote phosphaturia and suppress calcitriol.
(b) Klotho — membrane-bound co-receptor required for FGF-23/FGFR1 complex formation.

Q22Endocrine

A patient has low TSH, low ACTH, low T3/T4, and low cortisol. A hypothalamic mass compresses the pituitary stalk, disrupting the hypothalamic-portal system.
(a) What happens to hypothalamic releasing hormones (TRH, CRH) levels?
(b) What happens to their concentration in portal blood reaching the anterior pituitary?

✦ Answer
(a) Hypothalamic releasing hormones increase — the hypothalamus upregulates secretion due to loss of negative feedback, but cannot deliver hormones because the stalk is blocked.
(b) Portal blood hormone concentration decreases — physical disruption of the portal vessels prevents delivery to the anterior pituitary.
Q23Endocrine

A woman has amenorrhoea, decreased libido, and galactorrhoea. Labs: elevated prolactin, low FSH and LH, pituitary microadenoma.
(a) What happens to GnRH secretion?
(b) Effect on ovulation?
(c) Effect on menstruation?

✦ Answer
(a) GnRH decreases — elevated prolactin inhibits hypothalamic GnRH pulsatile secretion.
(b) Anovulation.
(c) Amenorrhoea.
Q24Endocrine

FSH and LH are elevated, oestradiol is 20 pg/mL (low), but the ovaries do not respond.
(a) What happens to androstenedione production by the ovaries?
(b) What happens to androstenedione from the adrenal cortex?

✦ Answer
(a) Ovarian androstenedione decreases — ovaries are unresponsive (premature ovarian failure/receptor defect); theca cells cannot respond to LH.
(b) Adrenal androstenedione unchanged (normal) — adrenal androgen secretion is ACTH-dependent, not gonadotropin-dependent.
Q25Endocrine

During normal pregnancy, thyroid function changes.
(a) What happens to total T3 and T4 levels?
(b) Does the pregnant woman manifest clinical hyperthyroidism?

✦ Answer
(a) Total T3 and T4 increase — oestrogen raises TBG (thyroid-binding globulin), increasing total bound hormone, but free T3/T4 remain normal.
(b) No — the woman is clinically euthyroid because free hormone levels and TSH are normal.
Q26Endocrine

A man with andropause presents with low testosterone, but LH is not elevated as expected. At which level of the hormonal axis is the dysfunction primarily located?

✦ Answer
Hypothalamus (or hypothalamus-pituitary axis) — failure to elevate LH despite low testosterone indicates the defect is central (secondary hypogonadism), specifically impaired GnRH pulsatile secretion from the hypothalamus.

Q28Endocrine

In type 2 DM, sulfonylurea drugs increase insulin secretion.
(a) How does the drug affect ATP-dependent K⁺ channels (K_ATP) in β-cells?
(b) What is the subsequent effect on voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels?

✦ Answer
(a) Sulfonylureas close K_ATP channels (bind SUR1 subunit) → membrane depolarises.
(b) Depolarisation opens voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels → Ca²⁺ influx → insulin exocytosis.

Q30Endocrine🏥 Path

A patient has a parathyroid adenoma with excess PTH secretion.
(a) What happens to bone calcium?
(b) What happens to serum calcium?

✦ Answer
(a) Bone calcium decreases — PTH activates osteoclasts → bone resorption.

Q31Endocrine🏥 Path

A patient has: elevated serum calcium, low urinary calcium, asymptomatic, normal or elevated PTH, impaired calcium sensing. Where does the causative genetic mutation primarily occur?

✦ Answer
The calcium-sensing receptor (CaSR) gene — loss-of-function mutation. The parathyroid and kidney misread normal Ca²⁺ as low, causing inappropriate PTH secretion and reduced urinary Ca²⁺ excretion. Diagnosis: Familial Hypocalciuric Hypercalcaemia (FHH).
Q32Endocrine🏥 Path

Which hormone is primarily responsible for increasing serum calcium? (via bone resorption, renal Ca²⁺ reabsorption, and stimulation of calcitriol synthesis)

✦ Answer
Parathyroid hormone (PTH).
Q33Endocrine

Which plasma protein transports both thyroid hormones (T3/T4) AND vitamin A in the blood?

✦ Answer
Transthyretin (pre-albumin) — transports T3/T4 and forms a complex with retinol-binding protein (RBP) to carry vitamin A (retinol). Note: Thyroglobulin is only intracellular in the thyroid follicle.
Q34Endocrine🩸 Haem

An astronaut on a space station performs regular exercise in microgravity. What happens to red blood cell (RBC) mass?

✦ Answer
RBC mass decreases — in microgravity, cephalad fluid shift triggers plasma volume redistribution; the body reduces erythropoietin and erythropoiesis → "space anaemia."

Neurophysiology & Special Senses 9 Qs
Q35Neurophysiology

What type of electrical conduction is associated with graded potentials, as opposed to the all-or-none conduction of action potentials?

✦ Answer
Electrotonic (decremental/passive) conduction — graded potentials spread via local ionic currents and decay with distance (no voltage-gated Na⁺ channels, no refractory period, amplitude proportional to stimulus strength).
Q36Neurophysiology

A strong stimulus is applied but does not always trigger an action potential. What are two other factors that determine whether an action potential is generated?

✦ Answer
1. Membrane excitability — during absolute/relative refractory periods, another AP cannot be generated regardless of stimulus strength.
2. Distance from the axon hillock — EPSPs must spatially and temporally summate at the axon initial segment to reach threshold; distal inputs lose amplitude via cable properties.
Q37Neurophysiology

Rhodopsin is a visual pigment with a 7-transmembrane G-protein coupled structure. What is the name of the chromophore it contains?

✦ Answer
11-cis retinal — photon absorption isomerises it to all-trans retinal, triggering opsin conformational change → transducin activation → phototransduction cascade.
Q38Neurophysiology📊 Graph

The graph shows mean steady-state firing rate (impulses/sec) vs. skin temperature (°C) for two cutaneous receptor types. Curve I peaks at ~40–45°C; Curve II peaks at ~20–25°C. Which curve represents warm thermoreceptors?

✦ Answer
Curve I — warm thermoreceptors (TRPV1/C-fibres) peak at ~40–45°C. Curve II represents cold receptors (TRPM8) peaking at ~20–25°C.
Q39Neurophysiology

The horizontal semicircular canal detects angular acceleration via the crista ampullaris. Which otolith organ (not in the ampulla) detects static head position and linear acceleration in the horizontal plane?

✦ Answer
Utricle (macula utriculi) — contains calcium carbonate otoliths on hair cells; detects horizontal linear acceleration and static head tilt due to gravity. (The saccule detects vertical linear acceleration.)
Q40Neurophysiology

After stopping rotation, continued endolymph movement causes involuntary rhythmic eye movement. (a) What is this phenomenon called? (b) Which cochlear fluid compartment (scala) receives vibrations directly from the oval window via the stapes?

✦ Answer
(a) Post-rotatory nystagmus — slow phase in the direction of previous rotation, fast phase opposite.
(b) Scala vestibuli — the oval window opens into the scala vestibuli (perilymph), which transmits pressure waves to the scala tympani via the helicotrema.
Q41Autonomic🏥 Path

An adrenergic agonist eye drop is administered, causing pupillary dilation (mydriasis).
(a) Which muscle is responsible for pupillary dilation?
(b) What type of adrenergic receptor mediates its contraction?

✦ Answer
(a) Radial (dilator) muscle of the iris (dilator pupillae).
(b) Alpha-1 (α₁) adrenergic receptor — sympathetic activation → α₁ → radial muscle contraction → mydriasis.

✦ Answer

2. Miosis — pupillary constriction (loss of dilator pupillae α₁ innervation)
3. Anhidrosis — absent ipsilateral facial sweating (loss of sudomotor sympathetic supply)

Immunology 3 Qs
Q43Immunology

During T-cell development in the thymus: developing T-cells that recognise self-antigen complexes undergo apoptosis.
(a) What is this selection process called?
(b) What disease condition is prevented by this process?

✦ Answer
(a) Negative selection — high-affinity autoreactive T-cells are deleted in the thymic medulla via clonal deletion.
(b) Autoimmune disease — without this, self-reactive T-cells escape to the periphery and attack host tissues.
Q44Immunology

T-cells can only be activated by antigen-presenting cells (APCs) when both TCR binding (signal 1) AND co-stimulation (signal 2) occur. What state does the T-cell enter if TCR binds antigen but co-stimulation is absent?

✦ Answer
Anergy — the T-cell becomes permanently unresponsive (functionally paralysed); a peripheral tolerance mechanism that prevents autoimmunity when self-antigens are presented without costimulatory signals (CD28/B7 interaction absent).
P1: "what state does the t cells enter if … absent of costimulation → energy state" (mishearing: "energy" = anergy)
Q45Immunology

Shortly after acute inflammation begins, neutrophils are mobilised and must cross the vascular endothelium.
(a) Which cell adhesion molecules mediate neutrophil rolling and firm adhesion?
(b) What type of movement do neutrophils use during extravasation (diapedesis)?

✦ Answer
(a) Rolling: Selectins (P-selectin, E-selectin on endothelium; L-selectin on neutrophil). Firm adhesion: Integrins (LFA-1/ICAM-1 interaction).
(b) Transendothelial migration (diapedesis) via PECAM-1 (CD31) at endothelial junctions.

Gastrointestinal 3 Qs
Q46GI

A specific cell type generates the slow-wave electrical pacemaker activity coordinating GI motility. Its disruption leads to delayed gastric emptying. Which type of cell is this?

✦ Answer
Interstitial cells of Cajal (ICC) — located in the myenteric plexus and circular muscle, they function as pacemakers of the GI tract. Loss or dysfunction causes gastroparesis (delayed gastric emptying).
Q47GI

H. pylori secretes urease to convert urea → NH₃ + CO₂, buffering gastric acid. Which organ synthesises the primary substrate (urea) that H. pylori uses?

✦ Answer
The liver — urea is synthesised in hepatocytes via the urea cycle (ornithine cycle), then enters circulation and diffuses into gastric juice.
Q48GI

Cholera toxin permanently activates Gs, elevating cAMP in intestinal epithelial cells → massive secretory diarrhoea. Which ion channel is constitutively opened by elevated cAMP, causing Cl⁻ secretion?

✦ Answer
CFTR (Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator) — cAMP activates PKA, phosphorylates CFTR → opens Cl⁻ channel → Cl⁻ efflux → Na⁺ and H₂O follow osmotically → massive watery diarrhoea. The ion is Cl⁻.

Haematology & Biochemistry 3 Qs
Q52Biochemistry🏥 Path

Red blood cells are protected from reactive oxygen species (ROS) via the G6PD pathway generating NADPH. What is the role of NADPH in protecting RBCs from oxidative damage?

✦ Answer
NADPH is used by glutathione reductase to reduce oxidised glutathione (GSSG → GSH). Reduced glutathione (GSH) then acts as a ROS scavenger via glutathione peroxidase (H₂O₂ → H₂O). In G6PD deficiency, insufficient NADPH → RBC oxidative haemolysis.
Q53Biochemistry🏥 Path📋 Reaction

The diagram shows the oxidation of succinic acid to fumaric acid. Coenzyme Y accepts 2 hydrogen atoms (−2H) and becomes reduced. Which vitamin is the precursor of coenzyme Y?

✦ Answer
Vitamin B₂ (Riboflavin) — FAD (flavin adenine dinucleotide) is derived from riboflavin. It accepts 2H in the succinate dehydrogenase reaction (Krebs cycle / Complex II), becoming FADH₂.
Q54Haematology🏥 Path

Briefly describe the basic pathophysiology of sickling in sickle cell disease.

✦ Answer
A point mutation (Glu→Val at position 6 of the β-globin chain) produces HbS. Under low O₂ conditions, HbS polymerises → sickle-shaped RBCs → vaso-occlusion, haemolysis, and ischaemic organ damage.

Pathophysiology & Clinical Scenarios 9 Qs
Q55Pathophysiology🩸 Haem

A marathon runner collapses on a hot day: confusion, hypotension, core temperature 41°C, prolonged PT, elevated plasma myoglobin, widespread microthrombosis, haemorrhage, multi-organ dysfunction. What is the pathophysiological complication causing both multi-organ failure AND uncontrolled bleeding?

✦ Answer
Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) — exertional heat stroke triggers massive tissue damage → cytokine storm → simultaneous activation of coagulation (microthrombi) and consumption of clotting factors (haemorrhage).
Q56Pathophysiology

Which phase of micturition involves contraction of the detrusor muscle?

✦ Answer
The voiding (micturition) phase — coordinated parasympathetic activation (muscarinic M3 receptors) causes detrusor contraction combined with relaxation of the internal and external urethral sphincters.
Q57Pathophysiology

A patient has vasocongestion but fails to achieve orgasm, remaining in a sustained state of arousal. Which phase of the sexual response cycle does this represent?

✦ Answer
Phase II — Plateau phase (Masters & Johnson model: Excitement → Plateau → Orgasm → Resolution). Vasocongestion is sustained without progression to orgasm.
Q58Pathophysiology

A scuba diver ascends rapidly to the surface without proper decompression stops and develops dizziness and joint pain. What condition is the diver experiencing?

✦ Answer
Decompression sickness (DCS / "the bends") — rapid pressure decrease causes dissolved nitrogen to come out of solution, forming gas bubbles in tissues and blood → joint pain, neurological symptoms, cardiovascular collapse.
Q59Pathophysiology🫁 Resp

During diving at a high partial pressure of O₂ (PO₂ = 225 mmHg). Which component of blood oxygen carriage increases most significantly at this elevated PO₂?

✦ Answer
Oxygen dissolved in plasma — at PO₂ = 225 mmHg, Hb is already ~100% saturated (cannot carry more). Additional O₂ dissolves physically in plasma (Henry's law: dissolved O₂ ∝ PO₂ × 0.003 mL/dL/mmHg). This principle underlies hyperbaric oxygen therapy.
Q60Pathophysiology🛡️ Immuno

A patient develops fever. A bacterial component elevates the hypothalamic temperature set-point. What specific type of bacterial toxin/component is classified as an exogenous pyrogen?

✦ Answer
Endotoxin (LPS, lipopolysaccharide) from gram-negative bacteria — stimulates macrophages/monocytes to release endogenous pyrogens (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α → PGE₂ in hypothalamus → raises set-point).
Q61Pathophysiology⚗️ Endo

A patient has worsening vision, arterial stiffness, and elevated HbA1c (type 2 DM). One process involves a non-enzymatic reaction between glucose and a protein, leading to AGE (Advanced Glycation End-product) formation and functional decline. Which amino acid serves as the initial site of this non-enzymatic glycation reaction?

✦ Answer
Lysine — the ε-amino group of lysine residues forms a Schiff base with glucose → Amadori rearrangement → irreversible AGEs → protein cross-linking → retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy.
P1: "Lisin" confirmed · P2: "glisin" (mishearing)
Q62Pathophysiology

One characteristic of ageing is a change in protein turnover rate.
(a) How does protein turnover change with ageing?
(b) What is the consequence for damaged/misfolded proteins?

✦ Answer
(a) Protein turnover rate increases (per competition notes).
(b) Despite increased turnover attempts, damaged protein accumulation increases — proteasomal and autophagic efficiency decline with age, so clearance cannot keep pace with misfolded protein generation.
Q63Pathophysiology⚗️ Endo

A patient presents with asymptomatic hypercalcaemia, normal or elevated PTH, and very low urinary calcium. This rare condition involves impaired calcium sensing. What is the diagnosis and the genetic mutation site?

✦ Answer
Familial Hypocalciuric Hypercalcaemia (FHH) — caused by loss-of-function mutation in the Calcium-Sensing Receptor (CaSR) gene, causing inappropriate parathyroid function and reduced renal Ca²⁺ excretion.
