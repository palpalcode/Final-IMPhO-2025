Chapter 54 — Section IX

THE MALE REPRODUCTIVE SYSTEM

Physiology, Endocrinology & Pathophysiology — IMSPQ / IPO Level
HIGH-YIELD BIG PICTURE: The male reproductive system is not merely about producing sperm. It is a highly regulated endocrine-neuro-gonadal network that integrates:
  • Gametogenesis — continuous production of haploid spermatozoa from puberty until death
  • Steroidogenesis — testosterone synthesis for local (paracrine) and systemic (endocrine) actions
  • Delivery system — accessory glands and ducts that nourish, transport, and activate sperm
  • Neural-vascular coordination — precise autonomic and somatic control of erection, emission, and ejaculation
SYSTEM INTEGRATION Why must we view this as ONE system?

The testis performs two functions simultaneously: spermatogenesis (in seminiferous tubules, driven by FSH + testosterone) and steroidogenesis (in Leydig cells, driven by LH). These two compartments are anatomically distinct but functionally inseparable. Without testosterone from Leydig cells, spermatogenesis fails. Without Sertoli cell support, germ cells degenerate. The hypothalamus and pituitary integrate these processes via pulsatile GnRH, which represents a master clock that coordinates both arms. Disruption at any node — hypothalamus, pituitary, Leydig cells, Sertoli cells, ducts, or neural pathways — produces predictable, reasoning-derived clinical consequences.

Anatomy Foundation: Two Compartments, Two Functions

🔵 Seminiferous Tubules

  • Sertoli cells — nurse/support cells; form blood-testis barrier via tight junctions
  • Germ cells — spermatogonia → primary spermatocytes → secondary spermatocytes → spermatids → spermatozoa
  • Lumen — mature sperm released here (spermiation)
  • Basement membrane — supported by lamina propria externa

Immature cells at periphery; mature cells at lumen — this spatial gradient reflects temporal progression.

🩷 Interstitium (Leydig Cells)

  • Leydig cells — steroidogenic cells between tubules; produce ~95% of circulating testosterone
  • Rich vascular supply — hormones enter circulation immediately
  • LH receptors on plasma membrane → Gs-coupled

Leydig cells and Sertoli cells engage in bidirectional crosstalk via testosterone, estradiol, and growth factors.

IMPORTANT MECHANISM Why must the testis be in the scrotum?

The internal body temperature (~37°C) is too hot for spermatogenesis. The scrotum maintains testicular temperature at ~34-35°C via:

  • Cremasteric muscle — contracts to pull testis closer to body when cold; relaxes when warm
  • Pampiniform plexus — countercurrent heat exchange between warm arterial blood and cool venous blood

Pathophysiology link: Cryptorchidism (undescended testis) → sustained abdominal temperature → germ cell damage → infertility + increased risk of testicular cancer (maldescent is a major risk factor for seminoma).

COMMON TRAP Is cryptorchidism always bilateral? Does it always cause infertility?

Unilateral cryptorchidism may spare fertility if the contralateral testis is normal, BUT the undescended testis still carries cancer risk and should be orchidopexied before 2 years of age to preserve germ cells. The descended testis may also show subtle dysfunction due to shared underlying etiology (e.g., hormonal defect).

Core Principle: The HPG Axis as a Pulsatile Oscillator

HIGH-YIELD The entire male reproductive system is governed by a single principle: pulsatile GnRH release from the hypothalamus.

Why pulsatile? Because continuous GnRH causes DOWNREGULATION of GnRH receptors on gonadotrophs → desensitization → suppression of LH and FSH. This is the mechanism behind GnRH analogue therapy for prostate cancer ("chemical castration").

GnRH & Gonadotropin Structure: Molecular Details

HIGH-YIELD GnRH is synthesized as a 69-amino acid prohormone that is enzymatically cleaved to the mature decapeptide. Understanding this processing and the glycoprotein nature of LH/FSH is essential for olympiad-level reasoning.
BIOCHEMISTRY LINK

GnRH Biosynthesis and Processing

  • Gene: GNRH1 located on chromosome 8
  • Preprohormone: Synthesized as a 69-amino acid prepro-GnRH in hypothalamic neurons (arcuate nucleus, preoptic area)
  • Processing: Signal peptide cleavage → pro-GnRH → enzymatic cleavage by prohormone convertases at dibasic sites → mature decapeptide (10 amino acids: Gln-His-Trp-Ser-Tyr-Gly-Leu-Arg-Pro-Gly-NH₂)
  • Amidation: The C-terminal glycine is essential for receptor binding and bioactivity
  • Secretion: Released into hypophyseal portal system in pulsatile bursts (~8-14 pulses/24h in adult men)

Why this matters: Any mutation affecting the prohormone processing enzymes or the cleavage sites would abolish GnRH production → hypogonadotropic hypogonadism despite intact GnRH neurons.

LH and FSH: The Glycoprotein Hormone Family

LH and FSH are members of the glycoprotein hormone family that includes:

  • Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) — placental
  • Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) — pituitary

LHβ vs hCGβ — Critical Distinction

  • LHβ and hCGβ are identical in amino acid sequence EXCEPT:
  • hCGβ has an additional 24 amino acids at the C-terminus
  • hCGβ has additional glycosylation sites at the C-terminus
  • Result: hCG has a much longer half-life than LH (~24-36h vs ~20-30 min)
  • Biological activities are very similar — hCG can substitute for LH in most clinical settings

Clinical application: hCG is substituted for LH in treating hypogonadotropic hypogonadism because it is much more readily available and has longer duration of action. The identical β-subunit core means hCG binds LH receptors with high affinity.

Non-Placental hCG Production

Although hCG is primarily a placental hormone (Chapter 56), small amounts are also produced by:

  • Testes (Leydig/Sertoli cells)
  • Pituitary gland
  • Other non-placental tissues

Physiological significance: These local sources may contribute to paracrine regulation, but circulating hCG in non-pregnant males is negligible compared to LH.

Puberty: The Neurobiological Switch

HIGH-YIELD Puberty is not merely hormonal activation — it is the reawakening of a quiescent hypothalamic-pituitary system.

During fetal life and early infancy, the HPG axis is active ("mini-puberty" at 1-3 months). Then, the system enters a prepubertal quiescence due to heightened CNS inhibition of GnRH neurons. At puberty, this CNS "brake" is released — mechanism still not fully understood but involves kisspeptin neurons stimulating GnRH release.

CLINICAL REASONING

Tanner Stages — What Matters for Physiology?

Forget memorizing all Tanner stages. Focus on the physiological sequence:

  1. Testicular enlargement (Stage 2) — driven by FSH-stimulated Sertoli cell proliferation + LH-stimulated Leydig cell growth
  2. Pubic hair (adrenal androgens + testicular androgens) — DHT-dependent; requires 5α-reductase in skin/hair follicles
  3. Penile growth (length first, then girth) — testosterone/DHT-dependent
  4. Pubertal growth spurt (Stage 3-4) — GH + testosterone synergy; boys peak later than girls (~14 vs 12 years)
  5. Spermarchy (~13.4 years) — first sperm in urine; corresponds to genital stages 3-4
COMMON TRAP Why does the pubertal growth spurt occur LATER in boys than girls?

Estrogen (from peripheral aromatization of androgens) accelerates epiphyseal maturation. Boys have lower estrogen exposure early in puberty compared to girls, so their growth plates remain open longer. Testosterone itself stimulates GH/IGF-1 but also eventually converts to estrogen → epiphyseal closure. The trade-off: later puberty → longer growth period → greater adult height.

Gonadotropin Secretion Across the Lifespan

HIGH-YIELD The pattern of gonadotropin secretion changes dramatically across development — from fetal activation to postnatal surge, prepubertal quiescence, and pubertal reactivation. Understanding these transitions is key to diagnosing disorders of sexual development.
DEVELOPMENTAL TIMELINE

Fetal Period

  • End of first trimester: Pituitary gonadotrophs are functional
  • Gonadotropin secretion: Rises rapidly → reaches plateau
  • Late fetal life: Begins to decline due to rising sex steroid negative feedback from fetal testes

Postnatal "Mini-Puberty" (1-3 months)

  • Transient surge of LH and testosterone occurs in male infants
  • Critical point: This surge is clearly independent of sex steroids — it is driven by CNS maturation and release of the prepubertal "brake"
  • Functional significance: Male primates release LH in response to GnRH administration at 1-3 months — proving functional competence of the anterior pituitary
  • Duration: Short-lived; system returns to quiescence within months

Prepubertal Period

  • FSH > LH during most of the prepubertal period
  • Why FSH predominates? The immature testis produces minimal inhibin (which specifically suppresses FSH). Without inhibin, FSH is relatively unchecked.
  • System remains quiescent due to CNS inhibition of GnRH neurons — mechanism involves GABAergic and opioidergic tone

Pubertal Transition

  • Pattern reverses: LH > FSH after puberty
  • Why the reversal? As testes mature, Sertoli cells begin producing inhibin — a specific inhibitor of FSH secretion at the pituitary level. Inhibin selectively suppresses FSH, allowing LH to become the dominant gonadotropin.
  • Increased pituitary sensitivity: The gonadotrophs become more responsive to GnRH, and preferential LH release is established
OLYMPAD FAVORITE Why does the FSH > LH pattern in prepubertal boys matter clinically?

In prepubertal hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (e.g., Kallmann syndrome), the hormonal profile shows low LH AND low FSH. However, because FSH is normally higher than LH in childhood, the absolute FSH level may appear "less low" than LH. This can obscure the diagnosis if one only looks at the adult reference ranges. Always interpret pediatric hormone levels using age-appropriate norms.

MECHANISM

Inhibin — The FSH-Specific Brake

  • Structure: Heterodimeric glycoprotein (α + β subunits) belonging to the TGF-β superfamily
  • Source: Sertoli cells in testis; granulosa cells in ovary
  • Target: Anterior pituitary gonadotrophs ONLY — does NOT act on hypothalamus
  • Action: Specifically suppresses FSH synthesis and secretion without affecting LH
  • Mechanism: Inhibin antagonizes activin signaling in gonadotrophs; activin normally stimulates FSHβ gene transcription

Why this matters: The selective FSH suppression by inhibin explains why FSH > LH in prepuberty (low inhibin) and why LH > FSH in adulthood (high inhibin from mature Sertoli cells). It also explains why Sertoli cell damage causes selective FSH elevation.

Androgen Action: Genomic vs Non-Genomic

HIGH-YIELD Androgens are NOT just "male hormones." They are pleiotropic steroids acting on nearly every tissue via:
  • Genomic pathway (primary): Testosterone/DHT → nuclear androgen receptor (AR) → homodimerization → binding to androgen response elements (AREs) → gene transcription → protein synthesis
  • Non-genomic pathway (minor but important): Membrane-initiated signaling → rapid activation of kinases, ion channels

🔵 Testosterone (T)

  • Major circulating androgen (~95% from testes)
  • Binds AR with moderate affinity
  • Can be converted to:
    • DHT (via 5α-reductase) — 30-50x more potent
    • Estradiol (via aromatase) — acts via ER

🩷 Dihydrotestosterone (DHT)

  • Formed peripherally in target tissues
  • Required for: external genitalia development (fetal), prostate growth, male-pattern hair growth, temporal recession
  • NOT required for: muscle anabolism, bone growth, spermatogenesis (these use testosterone directly)
MECHANISM

5α-Reductase Deficiency — The Classic Olympiad Problem

Genetics: Autosomal recessive; deficiency of 5α-reductase type 2 (SRD5A2 gene).

Phenotype at birth: Ambiguous genitalia (46,XY) — testosterone is normal but cannot be converted to DHT in external genital tissues → undermasculinized genitalia.

Puberty: Massive virilization occurs! Why? Because:

  1. Testosterone levels rise at puberty
  2. Testosterone itself can bind AR (though weakly) → sufficient for muscle, voice, spermatogenesis
  3. The internal structures (Wolffian ducts) developed normally because they depend on testosterone, NOT DHT

Key insight: This proves that DHT is essential for external genital development but NOT for Wolffian duct differentiation or pubertal virilization — a beautiful natural experiment in steroid physiology.

StAR — The Molecular Key

Cholesterol is the substrate, but it cannot cross the mitochondrial outer membrane spontaneously. The problem: P-450scc resides on the inner mitochondrial membrane (IMS side).

StAR solution:

  1. 37-kDa pro-StAR ferries cholesterol from ER to outer mitochondrial membrane
  2. 30-kDa mature StAR enters mitochondrial intermembrane space
  3. StAR extracts cholesterol from outer membrane and deposits it on inner membrane
  4. P-450scc now has access to cholesterol → cleaves side chain → pregnenolone

Why this matters: Congenital lipoid adrenal hyperplasia (CLAH) — mutation in StAR → no steroidogenesis in ALL steroidogenic tissues (adrenal + gonads) → 46,XY female phenotype + adrenal insufficiency.

OLYMPAD FAVORITE Why is P-450c17 called a "bifunctional enzyme" and why does this matter?

P-450c17 has two activities: 17α-hydroxylase AND 17,20-lyase (desmolase). Both activities are required for androgen synthesis. In the Δ5 pathway (preferred in testis): pregnenolone → 17α-OH-pregnenolone → DHEA. In the Δ4 pathway: progesterone → 17α-OH-progesterone → androstenedione. The same protein catalyzes both hydroxylation and side-chain cleavage. Deficiency of 17α-hydroxylase/17,20-lyase → hypertension (mineralocorticoid excess) + sexual infantilism.

Steroidogenic Proteins: StAR and SCP-2

HIGH-YIELD Cholesterol transport into mitochondria is the rate-limiting step of steroidogenesis. TWO proteins collaborate: SCP-2 ferries cholesterol in the cytosol, and StAR extracts it across the mitochondrial membranes.
BIOCHEMISTRY LINK

SCP-2 (Sterol Carrier Protein-2) — The Cytosolic Ferry

  • Molecular weight: 13.5 kDa
  • Function: Transports cholesterol from the plasma membrane or organellar membranes to other organellar membranes, including the outer mitochondrial membrane
  • Mechanism: Binds cholesterol in hydrophobic pocket → diffuses through cytosol → delivers to outer mitochondrial membrane
  • Relationship with StAR: SCP-2 delivers cholesterol TO the outer membrane; StAR then extracts it and moves it across the intermembrane space to the inner membrane

Why both proteins are needed: SCP-2 handles cytosolic trafficking; StAR handles the mitochondrial membrane barrier. Without SCP-2, cholesterol accumulates in cytosol but cannot reach mitochondria efficiently. Without StAR, cholesterol reaches the outer membrane but cannot enter the mitochondria.

BIOCHEMISTRY LINK

  • Gene: STAR (also called STARD1)

    • 30-kDa mature StAR: Resides in mitochondrial intermembrane space
  • Action: Extracts cholesterol from outer mitochondrial membrane → ferries across intermembrane space → deposits on inner mitochondrial membrane where P-450scc resides

Congenital Lipoid Adrenal Hyperplasia (CLAH) — StAR Deficiency

Mechanism: Mutation in STAR gene → no functional StAR → cholesterol cannot enter mitochondria → no pregnenolone synthesis.

Phenotype (46,XY):

  • Normal male internal structures (Wolffian ducts developed before StAR was required — guided by hCG-driven T in first trimester)
  • Female external genitalia (no T/DHT for masculinization)
  • Adrenal insufficiency (no cortisol, no aldosterone) — life-threatening in neonatal period
  • "Lipoid" appearance: cholesterol accumulates in adrenal and Leydig cells (lipid-laden)

Key reasoning: The presence of Wolffian structures proves that some steroidogenesis occurred in first trimester (via hCG stimulation and possibly residual StAR activity or alternative cholesterol transport). After birth, with no hCG, all steroidogenesis collapses.

Negative Feedback: The Two-Loop Architecture

HIGH-YIELD The HPT axis has TWO distinct negative feedback loops — this is the key to understanding selective gonadotropin abnormalities.

🔵 Loop 1: Testosterone → LH

  • Site: Hypothalamus (reduces GnRH pulse frequency/amplitude) + Anterior pituitary (reduces gonadotroph sensitivity to GnRH)
  • Molecule: Testosterone (and estradiol derived from aromatization)
  • Effect: Both GnRH pulsatility AND LH response are dampened
  • Clinical correlate: Androgen abuse (anabolic steroids) → suppressed GnRH → low LH → testicular atrophy

🩷 Loop 2: Inhibin → FSH

  • Site: Anterior pituitary ONLY (not hypothalamus)
  • Molecule: Inhibin (glycoprotein from Sertoli cells)
  • Effect: Selective suppression of FSH secretion
  • Clinical correlate: Sertoli cell-only syndrome → no germ cells → reduced inhibin → elevated FSH (but LH/T normal)

🔄 REASONING CHECKPOINT #1 — HPG Axis & Puberty

Q1: A 16-year-old boy has delayed puberty. His testosterone is low, LH and FSH are both low. Where is the lesion?

Step 1: Low T with low gonadotropins = hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (secondary/tertiary).
Step 2: Differential: Kallmann syndrome (anosmia + GnRH deficiency), constitutional delay, pituitary tumor, hyperprolactinemia, hemochromatosis.
Step 3: If anosmia present → Kallmann (failed GnRH neuron migration from olfactory placode).
Step 4: Treatment: Pulsatile GnRH or hCG + FSH to induce puberty and spermatogenesis.

Q2: Why does testosterone replacement therapy suppress spermatogenesis in men?

Mechanism: Exogenous T provides negative feedback on hypothalamus (↓GnRH pulsatility) and pituitary (↓LH). Low LH → Leydig cell atrophy → intratesticular testosterone collapses. Since spermatogenesis requires high LOCAL T concentration (maintained by ABP in tubules), the drop in intratesticular T halts spermatogenesis despite normal serum T.
Trade-off: Systemic androgenization vs. testicular shutdown. This is why testosterone is NOT a male contraceptive (reversible but unreliable).

Q3: A boy with 5α-reductase deficiency is raised as female. At puberty, he virilizes. Explain the physiology.

Step 1: 5α-reductase converts T → DHT in target tissues. DHT is required for fetal external genital masculinization.
Step 2: At puberty, Leydig cells produce massive amounts of T under LH drive.
Step 3: Testosterone itself (even without conversion to DHT) can bind AR and produce: voice deepening, muscle growth, spermatogenesis (in testes, T is sufficient).
Step 4: Wolffian ducts developed normally because they depend on T, not DHT. Only external genitalia and prostate remain underdeveloped.
Clinical pearl: These individuals are often fertile if sperm can be retrieved, because spermatogenesis depends on T, not DHT.

COMMON TRAP Why does the testis prefer the Δ5 pathway (pregnenolone → DHEA) over the Δ4 pathway (progesterone → androstenedione)?

The 17,20-lyase activity of P-450c17 acts preferentially on 17α-hydroxypregnenolone (Δ5 substrate) rather than 17α-hydroxyprogesterone (Δ4 substrate) in the human testis. This is because the lyase activity requires phosphorylation of serine residues on P-450c17, which is favored when acting on Δ5 substrates. The adrenal cortex, in contrast, uses the Δ4 pathway more heavily for cortisol synthesis.

  • Wolffian ducts regress (need AR-mediated T action)
  • External genitalia remain female (need DHT action)
  • Breast development occurs (T aromatized to E2; no androgen antagonism at breast tissue)

Key reasoning: The presence of testes, elevated T, and female phenotype in the same individual proves that hormone production is intact but target tissue response is absent. This is the definition of hormone resistance.

Testosterone and the Aging Male

HIGH-YIELD Unlike female menopause (abrupt), male "andropause" is a gradual decline in testosterone starting ~age 30-40, accelerating after 60.
CLINICAL REASONING

Why is LH often NOT elevated in aging men with low T?

This is a critical diagnostic distinction:

  • In primary testicular failure, Leydig cells die → T falls → negative feedback lost → LH should rise (compensatory)
  • In aging men, T declines BUT LH remains normal or only mildly elevated

Conclusion: The problem is NOT just Leydig cell failure. There is also hypothalamic-pituitary dysfunction — the aging brain loses its ability to generate robust GnRH pulses. This is a mixed primary + secondary hypogonadism.

Consequences of low T in aging:

  • ↓ Bone formation (osteoporosis risk)
  • ↓ Muscle mass and strength (sarcopenia)
  • ↓ Libido and erectile function
  • ↓ Hematocrit (T stimulates erythropoiesin)
  • ↓ Facial hair growth

T stimulates renal erythropoietin production AND directly enhances bone marrow sensitivity to EPO. Sertoli Cell: The Blood-Testis Barrier & Spermiation

HIGH-YIELD The Sertoli cell is the ONLY somatic cell in the seminiferous epithelium. It forms the blood-testis barrier (BTB) via tight junctions — essential for immune privilege.
MECHANISM

Blood-Testis Barrier — Why It Exists

Germ cells express antigens that are recognized as "foreign" by the immune system because:

  • Meiosis produces novel haploid antigens not present in somatic cells
  • Spermatogonia first appear at puberty — AFTER central tolerance is established in fetal life

Structure: Tight junctions between adjacent Sertoli cells divide the tubule into:

  • Basal compartment — spermatogonia, preleptotene spermatocytes (outside barrier); exposed to blood-borne substances
  • Adluminal compartment — meiotic and post-meiotic cells (inside barrier); protected environment

Function: Prevents autoimmune attack on germ cells; maintains unique ionic/metabolic environment for meiosis.

CLINICAL REASONING

Sertoli Cell-Only Syndrome (Germinal Cell Aplasia)

Pathophysiology: Seminiferous tubules contain only Sertoli cells; complete absence of germ cells. Accounts for 10-30% of azoospermic male infertility.

  • Etiology: Genetic (single-gene defect) OR acquired (orchitis, alcoholism, toxins, radiation)
  • Hormonal profile: Testosterone and LH usually NORMAL (Leydig cells intact). FSH often ELEVATED due to reduced inhibin production from Sertoli cells.
  • Why FSH is elevated: Loss of negative feedback from inhibin AND possibly from germ cell-derived factors. However, FSH elevation is not universal — some patients have normal FSH, suggesting suboptimal inhibin secretion rather than complete absence.

Key distinction from Klinefelter: In SCO syndrome, T and LH are normal and the man is normally virilized. In Klinefelter (47,XXY), T is low, LH is high, and there may be eunuchoid proportions + gynecomastia.

Spermiation — Release of Mature Sperm

Spermatids are embedded in Sertoli cell cytoplasm throughout spermiogenesis. At the final stage, the Sertoli cell releases mature spermatozoa into the lumen. This process requires:

  • Breakdown of Sertoli-germ cell adhesions (ectoplasmic specializations)
  • Apical cytoplasmic retraction
  • Tubulobulbar complexes that remove excess cytoplasm from spermatids

Failure of spermiation → mature sperm retained in epithelium → infertility despite normal sperm production.

🔄 REASONING CHECKPOINT #2 — Spermatogenesis & Epididymis

Q1: A 30-year-old man has azoospermia. His testosterone is normal, LH is normal, but FSH is markedly elevated. Testicular biopsy shows seminiferous tubules lined only by Sertoli cells with no germ cells. Explain the hormonal profile.

Step 1: Normal T + normal LH → Leydig cells are intact. The problem is NOT in the interstitium.
Step 2: Elevated FSH → loss of negative feedback. Inhibin is produced by Sertoli cells and specifically suppresses FSH at the pituitary.
Step 3: Without germ cells, Sertoli cells may produce less inhibin OR the absolute number of Sertoli cells is reduced → less total inhibin → FSH rises.
Step 4: This is Sertoli Cell-Only syndrome. The man is normally virilized (normal T) but sterile (no germ cells).
Contrast: In Klinefelter syndrome, both Leydig and germ cells are abnormal → low T + high LH + high FSH.

Q2: Why does cryptorchidism cause infertility if the testis is surgically brought down before puberty?

Step 1: Abdominal temperature (~37°C) damages germ cells, particularly spermatogonia and early meiotic cells.
Step 2: Leydig cells are more temperature-resistant → testosterone production may be preserved.
Step 3: If orchidopexy is performed before age 2, germ cell preservation is much better because the critical stem cell pool is still intact.
Step 4: After age 2-3, irreversible germ cell loss occurs → even with normal position, fertility is compromised.
Additional risk: Undescended testis has 5-10x increased risk of testicular cancer (seminoma) regardless of surgery timing — requires lifelong surveillance.

Q3: Predict the effect of a selective FSH receptor knockout in males.

Prediction: Spermatogenesis would be severely impaired but NOT completely absent.
Reasoning: FSH stimulates Sertoli cell proliferation and ABP production. However, testosterone alone (from LH-driven Leydig cells) can support some degree of spermatogenesis. In fact, men with isolated FSH deficiency or FSH receptor mutations have small testes, low sperm counts, but may still be fertile. This proves that LH/testosterone is the MINIMAL requirement; FSH is the OPTIMIZER.
Hormonal profile: Normal LH, normal T, elevated FSH (no inhibin feedback), small testes, oligo/azoospermia.

Emission & Ejaculation: Sympathetic then Somatic

HIGH-YIELD Emission and ejaculation are often conflated but are physiologically distinct. Emission is autonomic; ejaculation is a somatic spinal reflex.
MECHANISM

Emission — Sympathetic Control

Emission is the movement of semen into the prostatic urethra. It requires:

  1. Peristaltic contraction of vas deferens and seminal vesicles (α-adrenergic, NE-mediated)
  2. Prostatic smooth muscle contraction (α-adrenergic)

Neural pathway: Thoracolumbar sympathetic (T10-L2) → hypogastric nerve → pelvic plexus → postganglionic fibers to vas deferens, seminal vesicles, prostate, bladder neck.

Pharmacology: α-adrenergic agonists (ephedrine, imipramine, midodrine) promote emission and prevent retrograde ejaculation. α-blockers (tamsulosin) can cause retrograde ejaculation by relaxing the bladder neck.

Q1: A 65-year-old man with BPH is started on tamsulosin (α1-blocker). He reports decreased ejaculate volume and "dry orgasm." Explain the mechanism.

Q2: Why does diabetic neuropathy cause erectile dysfunction before it causes ejaculatory problems?

Step 1: Erection requires fine autonomic coordination: parasympathetic vasodilation (NO-cGMP) + sympathetic withdrawal. Small nerve fibers (autonomic) are affected early in diabetes.
Step 2: Ejaculation is a somatic spinal reflex involving large motor fibers (pudendal nerve) and striated muscles. These are more resistant to diabetic neuropathy.
Step 3: Additionally, emission requires sympathetic contraction of smooth muscle. As diabetes progresses, this too fails → retrograde ejaculation or absent emission.
Sequence: ED (autonomic) → emission dysfunction (sympathetic) → ejaculatory failure (somatic, late) — reflecting the length-dependent nature of diabetic neuropathy (longest fibers affected first).

Q3: A man takes both sildenafil and nitroglycerin. Why is this dangerous?

Step 1: Sildenafil inhibits PDE5 → prolongs cGMP action → enhances NO-mediated vasodilation in penis.
Step 2: Nitroglycerin is converted to NO in vascular smooth muscle → raises cGMP → systemic vasodilation.
Step 3: Combined effect: massive, uncontrolled cGMP accumulation in systemic vasculature → profound hypotension → cardiovascular collapse.
Mechanism: Both drugs converge on the same second messenger (cGMP) in the same signaling pathway (NO-guanylyl cyclase). This is synergistic toxicity, not just additive effect.

Pathophysiology Integration: From Mechanism to Manifestation

HIGH-YIELD Every clinical syndrome in male reproduction can be predicted by identifying the exact node of failure in the HPG axis, testicular compartments, or neural-vascular axis.

Congenital Absence of Vas Deferens (CAVD) & Cystic Fibrosis

Mechanism: CFTR mutation → defective chloride transport in epithelial cells → thick, inspissated secretions → obstruction and atresia of Wolffian duct derivatives (vas deferens, epididymis, seminal vesicles).

Phenotype:

  • Azoospermia with LOW ejaculate volume (no seminal vesicle fluid)
  • Testes are NORMAL (spermatogenesis intact!)
  • Hormones are NORMAL (HPG axis unaffected)

Reasoning: This is a transport obstruction, not a production defect. ICSI with epididymal or testicular sperm can achieve fertility. This proves that the defect is post-testicular.

Olympiad insight: CAVD may be the ONLY manifestation of CF in some men. Always test for CFTR mutations in azoospermic men with low volume and normal hormones.

Post-Vasectomy Changes

Vasectomy interrupts the ductal system. Long-term effects:

  • Pressure buildup in epididymis → epididymal dilation, sperm granuloma formation
  • Autoimmune response: Sperm antigens leak into interstitium → antisperm antibodies in ~50-70% of men
  • Testicular changes (in ~20%): Interstitial fibrosis, thickened tubule walls, decreased Sertoli cells

Reversal implications: Even with successful vasovasostomy, fertility may be reduced due to epididymal dysfunction, antibody formation, and testicular fibrosis.

OLYMPAD FAVORITE Why does testicular torsion cause rapid infertility if not corrected within 6 hours?

Torsion twists the spermatic cord → compromises arterial inflow AND venous drainage. The sequence:

  1. Venous obstruction first (thin-walled, low pressure) → congestion, edema
  2. Arterial obstruction follows (thick-walled, high pressure) → ischemia
  3. Germ cells (spermatogonia, spermatocytes) are exquisitely sensitive to ischemia → apoptosis within hours
  4. Sertoli cells and Leydig cells are more resistant (better metabolic reserves)

After 6 hours: Irreversible germ cell death → even if blood flow restored, fibrosis and atrophy ensue. The contralateral testis may also be damaged by autoimmune mechanisms ("sympathetic orchidopathy").

High-Yield Reasoning Patterns & Expert Frameworks

HIGH-YIELD Master these transferable reasoning frameworks — they apply to endocrine axes beyond reproduction.

Framework 1: The "Three-Level Axis" Approach

For ANY endocrine problem, ask:

  1. Level 1 (Hypothalamus): Is the releasing hormone present and pulsatile? (GnRH, CRH, TRH)
  2. Level 2 (Pituitary): Is the trophic hormone responding? (LH, ACTH, TSH)
  3. Level 3 (Target): Is the end organ producing the final hormone? (Testis, Adrenal, Thyroid)

Rule: If Level 3 hormone is LOW and Level 2 hormone is HIGH → primary failure. If both are LOW → secondary/tertiary failure.

Framework 2: The "Local vs Systemic" Distinction

The testis produces hormones for TWO purposes:

  • Local (paracrine): High concentration in tubules (T via ABP, estradiol, growth factors) — supports spermatogenesis
  • Systemic (endocrine): Circulating T for muscle, bone, brain, erythropoiesis

Clinical application: A man can have normal serum T but intratesticular T may be low (e.g., varicocele, cryptorchidism) → infertility despite "normal" hormone levels. Conversely, exogenous T raises serum T but suppresses intratesticular T → infertility.

Framework 3: The "Enzyme Logic" for Steroidopathies

When analyzing congenital adrenal hyperplasia or steroidogenic defects:

  1. Identify which enzyme is deficient
  2. Determine what accumulates ABOVE the block (precursor excess)
  3. Determine what is deficient BELOW the block (hormone deficiency)
  4. Determine which pathway is SHUNTED (e.g., blocked cortisol → shunted to androgens in 21-hydroxylase deficiency)

Example — 17α-hydroxylase deficiency: Blocked cortisol and androgen synthesis → mineralocorticoids (DOC) accumulate → hypertension + hypokalemia + sexual infantilism.

🎯 Olympiad Simulation — Question 1

Q1. A 25-year-old man presents with infertility. Semen analysis shows azoospermia. Hormonal profile reveals:

  • Testosterone: 2.1 ng/mL (normal 3-10)
  • LH: 28 IU/L (normal 2-12)
  • FSH: 32 IU/L (normal 1-10)

Physical examination reveals tall stature, long legs, small firm testes (~3 mL each), and gynecomastia. Karyotype analysis is performed.

(a) What is the most likely karyotype, and which cellular mechanism during gametogenesis is primarily defective?

(b) Explain why LH is elevated despite low testosterone. Why doesn't the negative feedback loop normalize LH?

(c) Explain the pathophysiology of gynecomastia in this patient using steroid biochemistry.

(d) If this patient were treated with hCG injections, would spermatogenesis be restored? Explain your reasoning.

Step-by-Step Reasoning

(a) Karyotype and Defective Mechanism

Diagnosis: Klinefelter syndrome — 47,XXY.

  • The extra X chromosome is inactivated (Barr body), but some genes escape X-inactivation → phenotype
  • Primary defect: germ cell failure during meiosis. The extra X disrupts pairing and crossing over in prophase I → germ cell apoptosis → Sertoli cell-only-like tubules + Leydig cell hyperplasia (compensatory)
  • Small firm testes: fibrosis replacing germ cells

(b) Elevated LH despite Low Testosterone

This is primary testicular failure. The Leydig cells are damaged and cannot produce adequate testosterone despite maximal LH stimulation. However, the negative feedback loop requires testosterone (and estradiol) to suppress GnRH/LH. Since testosterone is low, the hypothalamus and pituitary "see" insufficient negative feedback and continue to drive LH secretion at high levels.

Analogy: It's like pressing the accelerator (LH) but the engine (Leydig cells) is broken. The driver keeps pressing harder because the car isn't moving (low T feedback).

(c) Gynecomastia Pathophysiology

Gynecomastia results from an imbalance between estrogen and androgen action on breast tissue.

  • In Klinefelter: Leydig cell hyperplasia produces testosterone, but Sertoli cells (and peripherally) convert more T to estradiol via aromatase
  • The ratio of estrogen:androgen is increased → breast tissue proliferation
  • Additionally, SHBG may be elevated in Klinefelter → more bound T, less free T → reduced androgen antagonism at breast tissue

(d) hCG Treatment — Would It Restore Spermatogenesis?

No. hCG acts like LH and would stimulate the remaining Leydig cells to produce more testosterone (may improve virilization and libido). However, spermatogenesis requires:

  1. Germ cells (which are absent due to meiotic failure)
  2. Functional Sertoli cells in adequate numbers

Without germ cells, no amount of hormonal stimulation can create sperm. This is fundamentally different from hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (where hCG + FSH CAN initiate spermatogenesis because the testis is structurally intact).

Exception: In mosaic forms (46,XY/47,XXY), some germ cells may persist, and TESE (testicular sperm extraction) + ICSI may achieve fertility.

🎯 Olympiad Simulation — Question 2

Q2. A pharmaceutical company develops a drug that selectively inhibits StAR protein in Leydig cells. A 30-year-old man takes this drug for 4 weeks.

(a) Predict the changes in serum testosterone, LH, and FSH after 1 week and after 4 weeks. Explain the temporal dynamics.

(b) Would intratesticular testosterone fall to the same extent as serum testosterone? Why is this distinction clinically important?

(c) Predict the effect on spermatogenesis and semen parameters after 8 weeks. Explain using the timeline of spermatogenesis.

(d) If the drug is stopped, how long until fertility is restored? Explain your reasoning.

Step-by-Step Reasoning

(a) Hormonal Changes Over Time

After 1 week:

  • Testosterone: ↓ moderately. Leydig cells have some stored cholesterol and residual StAR. Basal steroidogenesis continues at reduced rate.
  • LH: ↑ slightly. Falling T reduces negative feedback, but the hypothalamic-pituitary axis responds with a lag.
  • FSH: unchanged initially. Inhibin from Sertoli cells is still normal (unless T falls enough to impair Sertoli function).

After 4 weeks:

  • Testosterone: ↓↓ markedly. Chronic StAR inhibition blocks cholesterol transport to P-450scc → pregnenolone synthesis collapses.
  • LH: ↑↑ significantly. Sustained low T removes negative feedback → maximal GnRH/LH pulsatility.
  • FSH: ↑ moderately. As intratesticular T falls, Sertoli function declines → inhibin production drops → FSH rises.

(b) Intratesticular vs Serum Testosterone

Intratesticular T falls MORE than serum T. Here's why:

  • Serum T is maintained partly by adrenal androgens (weak contribution) and by residual basal Leydig activity
  • Intratesticular T is maintained by high local concentration via ABP. When StAR is inhibited, local synthesis collapses, and ABP has nothing to bind
  • The testis normally maintains T at 20-50x serum concentration. This gradient is lost first

Clinical importance: A man might have "low-normal" serum T but already be infertile because intratesticular T (the driver of spermatogenesis) has collapsed. This is why exogenous testosterone causes infertility despite normal serum levels.

(c) Spermatogenesis After 8 Weeks

Spermatogenesis requires ~74 days. At 8 weeks (56 days):

  • Spermatogonia and early primary spermatocytes that were present at drug onset have now attempted to progress
  • Without adequate intratesticular T, meiosis and spermiogenesis fail → germ cell apoptosis
  • Semen: severe oligospermia or azoospermia
  • Progressive motility: reduced (epididymal function also depends on androgens)

Mechanism: Sertoli cells require T for ABP production, tight junction maintenance, and metabolic support of germ cells. FSH alone cannot compensate for T deficiency.

(d) Recovery Timeline After Cessation

Minimum: 74 days for the FIRST mature sperm to appear in ejaculate.

Reasoning:

  1. Drug cessation → StAR function restored → cholesterol transport resumes → pregnenolone synthesis restarts
  2. However, the germ cells that were in meiotic prophase when drug started have already degenerated
  3. New spermatogonia must enter the pipeline: spermatogonial mitosis (16-18 days) → meiosis (24 days) → spermiogenesis (23 days) → epididymal transit (12-26 days)

Full recovery: 3-6 months for normal sperm counts because multiple "waves" of spermatogenesis must be re-established simultaneously.

🎯 Olympiad Simulation — Question 3

Q3. A 46,XY individual is born with ambiguous genitalia. At puberty, he undergoes significant virilization: voice deepens, muscle mass increases, phallus enlarges. However, he has minimal facial hair, no temporal recession, and a small prostate on ultrasound. His testes are descended and of normal size. Hormonal studies show:

  • Testosterone: 5.8 ng/mL (normal adult male range)
  • DHT: 0.08 ng/mL (markedly low)
  • LH: 6 IU/L (normal)
  • FSH: 4 IU/L (normal)

(a) Identify the specific enzyme defect and explain the molecular basis of the phenotype.

(b) Why does virilization occur at puberty despite the enzyme defect? Which androgen-mediated processes are testosterone-dependent vs DHT-dependent?

(c) Would this individual be fertile? Explain using the specific cellular requirements for spermatogenesis vs external genital development.

(d) A brother is found to have the same mutation but was raised female from birth. At age 14, he begins to virilize. What is the embryological explanation for why the external genitalia remained female at birth despite normal testosterone production by fetal Leydig cells?

Step-by-Step Reasoning

(a) Enzyme Defect and Molecular Basis

Diagnosis: 5α-reductase type 2 deficiency (SRD5A2 mutation).

  • 5α-reductase converts testosterone → DHT in target tissues
  • DHT has 30-50x higher affinity for AR than testosterone
  • Normal serum T but low DHT → tissues requiring DHT are underdeveloped; tissues responsive to T are relatively preserved
  • Normal LH/FSH because testosterone negative feedback is intact (T is normal!)

(b) Pubertal Virilization — T vs DHT Dependencies

Why virilization at puberty? Testosterone levels rise massively at puberty under LH drive. Testosterone itself binds AR (weakly but sufficiently) to produce:

  • ✓ Muscle growth (anabolic)
  • ✓ Voice deepening (laryngeal growth)
  • ✓ Bone growth and epiphyseal closure (via aromatization to estrogen)
  • ✓ Spermatogenesis (intratesticular T is sufficient)
  • ✓ Wolffian duct development (occurred in fetal life)

What fails (DHT-dependent):

  • ✗ Prostate growth (small prostate)
  • ✗ Facial/body hair (minimal)
  • ✗ Temporal recession (none)
  • ✗ External genital masculinization (ambiguous at birth)

Embryological proof: This natural experiment demonstrates that fetal external genital development and prostate growth are DHT-dependent, whereas Wolffian duct differentiation and pubertal somatic virilization are testosterone-dependent.

(c) Fertility Potential

YES, potentially fertile.

  • Spermatogenesis depends on testosterone, not DHT. Intratesticular T is high (ABP-bound), and spermatogenesis proceeds normally
  • Sperm may be present in ejaculate (if genital anatomy permits delivery)
  • However, semen volume may be reduced (prostate and seminal vesicles are underdeveloped)
  • Obstructive issues may occur due to abnormal ejaculatory duct anatomy

Key reasoning: The testis itself is functionally normal in its endocrine and gametogenic roles. The defect is in peripheral target tissue activation (DHT formation). This is a post-receptor, tissue-specific defect — not a testicular failure.

(d) Embryological Explanation for Ambiguous Genitalia at Birth

During weeks 8-12 of gestation, fetal Leydig cells produce testosterone under hCG stimulation. This testosterone:

  • Stabilizes Wolffian ducts → epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles (T-dependent — NORMAL in this patient)
  • Is converted to DHT in genital skin and prostate (DHT-dependent — DEFECTIVE)

Without DHT, the genital tubercle, urethral folds, and labioscrotal swellings cannot masculinize → female-appearing or ambiguous external genitalia despite normal internal male structures.

At puberty: The surge in testosterone partially overcomes the DHT deficiency via direct AR binding → phallic enlargement and somatic virilization, but never complete external genital masculinization.

🎯 Olympiad Simulation — Question 4

Q4. A 55-year-old man with type 2 diabetes mellitus and peripheral neuropathy reports progressive erectile dysfunction over 2 years. He initially responded to sildenafil but now has minimal improvement. He also notes decreased ejaculate volume and "dribbling" emission.

(a) Explain the neurophysiological basis of his erectile dysfunction. Why did sildenafil lose efficacy?

(b) Explain the "dribbling emission." Which autonomic division is failing, and what is the anatomical correlate?

(c) If he develops complete retrograde ejaculation, which pharmacological agent would be most appropriate, and what is its mechanism?

(d) A fellow physician suggests testosterone replacement to improve his erectile function. Using physiological reasoning, explain why this might worsen rather than improve his fertility status.

Step-by-Step Reasoning

(a) ED Pathophysiology and Sildenafil Failure

Diabetic neuropathy affects small unmyelinated autonomic fibers before large somatic fibers. The parasympathetic cavernous nerves (derived from pelvic nerve S2-S4) are damaged → reduced NO release from nerve terminals AND reduced ACh-mediated endothelial NO synthesis.

Why sildenafil fails: Sildenafil inhibits PDE5 to prolong cGMP action, but cGMP is only generated when NO is present. With advanced neuropathy, NO production is so severely reduced that there is insufficient cGMP to prolong. Sildenafil cannot create erection from nothing — it can only amplify an existing signal.

Alternative: Direct intracavernosal injection of alprostadil (PGE1) or vacuum erection device (mechanical, bypasses neural pathway).

(b) Dribbling Emission — Sympathetic Failure

Emission requires sympathetic-mediated contraction of:

  • Vas deferens (α-adrenergic)
  • Seminal vesicles (α-adrenergic)
  • Prostate (α-adrenergic)
  • Internal urethral sphincter/bladder neck (α-adrenergic — CRITICAL)

Diabetic autonomic neuropathy damages sympathetic fibers traveling in hypogastric nerve (T10-L2 origin). Result:

  • Weak peristaltic contractions → poor propulsion of semen
  • Bladder neck fails to constrict → semen may flow backward into bladder OR dribble forward weakly

Anatomical correlate: The internal urethral sphincter (smooth muscle at bladder neck) is under sympathetic α-adrenergic control. Its failure converts coordinated emission into uncoordinated dribbling.

(c) Pharmacological Treatment for Retrograde Ejaculation

Best choice: Imipramine (a tricyclic antidepressant) or midodrine (α1-agonist) or pseudoephedrine.

Mechanism of imipramine:

  • Blocks norepinephrine reuptake at sympathetic nerve terminals → increased NE concentration in synaptic cleft
  • Enhanced α-adrenergic signaling at bladder neck → sphincter contraction
  • Also has anticholinergic effects that may reduce bladder detrusor overactivity

Mechanism of midodrine: Direct α1-adrenergic agonist → constricts bladder neck smooth muscle.

(d) Why Testosterone Replacement May Worsen Fertility

Even if fertility is not the patient's goal, understanding the mechanism is crucial:

  1. Exogenous testosterone provides negative feedback on hypothalamus → ↓ GnRH pulsatility
  2. ↓ GnRH → ↓ LH secretion from pituitary
  3. ↓ LH → Leydig cell atrophy → intratesticular testosterone collapses
  4. Intratesticular T (not serum T!) is required for spermatogenesis via ABP-mediated concentration in tubules
  5. Result: complete suppression of spermatogenesis → azoospermia

Paradox: Giving the "male hormone" makes the man infertile. This is because the testis requires PULSATILE LH drive to maintain local androgen environment. Exogenous T bypasses this local need.

Clinical application: This is the basis for male hormonal contraception research (T + progestin to suppress GnRH).

🔄 FINAL REASONING CHECKPOINT — Full Integration

Q1: A patient with a pituitary macroadenoma has low testosterone, low LH, and low FSH. After surgical removal of the tumor, gonadotropins remain low. However, administration of pulsatile GnRH restores normal LH, FSH, and testosterone. Where was the lesion?

Answer: The lesion was at the pituitary level (secondary hypogonadism), NOT the hypothalamus.

Reasoning: If the hypothalamus were damaged, pulsatile GnRH would not restore function because the hypothalamus IS the source of GnRH. The fact that exogenous GnRH works proves that the gonadotrophs are intact and responsive — they were simply not receiving GnRH due to pituitary stalk compression or destruction by the macroadenoma. This is the principle behind the GnRH stimulation test in distinguishing hypothalamic from pituitary disease.

Q2: In androgen insensitivity syndrome (AIS, 46,XY), testes produce normal testosterone, but target tissues cannot respond due to AR mutation. Predict: (a) LH level, (b) Testosterone level, (c) Breast development at puberty, (d) Internal genitalia.

(a) LH: ELEVATED. No negative feedback because target tissues cannot respond to T (resistance). The hypothalamus "sees" low effective androgen → increases GnRH drive.
(b) Testosterone: ELEVATED. High LH drives Leydig cells maximally. T may be 2-3x normal.
(c) Breast development: PRESENT (gynecomastia). High T is aromatized to estradiol. Breast tissue has normal ER and responds to estrogen. Without androgen antagonism (AR defective), estrogen action is unopposed → breast development.
(d) Internal genitalia: NO Wolffian structures (no AR response → no stabilization), NO Müllerian structures (AMH from Sertoli cells causes regression). Result: short blind-ending vagina, no uterus, testes in abdomen or inguinal canal.

Q3: Why does the testis produce estradiol, and why is this not "feminizing" in normal men?

Source: ~40% from testicular aromatization (Sertoli cells convert T → E2); ~60% from peripheral aromatization (adipose, skin, brain).

Why not feminizing:
1. The absolute amount is small (~20-50 pg/mL vs. 100-400 pg/mL in women)
2. Androgen action normally DOMINATES over estrogen in most tissues due to higher AR expression and more potent ligands (T, DHT)
3. Estrogen has physiological roles in men: bone mineralization, lipid profile, feedback on GnRH (modulates pulse frequency), and brain function

Pathology: When the estrogen:androgen ratio rises (obesity, liver disease, aromatase excess, AIS), gynecomastia and hypogonadism ensue.

Key Takeaways — The Non-Negotiable Concepts

HIGH-YIELD
  1. Pulsatile GnRH is the master switch. Continuous GnRH suppresses gonadotropins via receptor downregulation. This is the basis for chemical castration.
  2. LH and FSH use the same second messenger (cAMP/PKA) in target cells but produce different effects because the target cells are different. Do not confuse GnRH signaling (Gq/IP3) with LH/FSH signaling (Gs/cAMP).
  3. StAR is rate-limiting for ALL steroidogenesis. Without StAR, cholesterol cannot reach P-450scc. This is why CLAH affects adrenal AND gonads.
  4. Intratesticular testosterone (not serum testosterone) drives spermatogenesis. ABP maintains this high local concentration. Exogenous T raises serum but crashes intratesticular T → infertility.
  5. Inhibin selectively suppresses FSH; testosterone suppresses both LH and FSH (but mainly LH). This two-loop architecture explains all selective gonadotropin abnormalities.
  6. DHT is required for fetal external genital development and prostate growth, but NOT for Wolffian ducts, spermatogenesis, or pubertal muscle growth. This explains 5α-reductase deficiency.
  7. Erection = NO → cGMP → smooth muscle relaxation. Emission = sympathetic α-adrenergic contraction. Ejaculation = somatic spinal reflex. Each has distinct neural control and distinct drug targets.
  8. The blood-testis barrier exists because germ cell antigens appear after self-tolerance is established. Breakdown causes autoimmune orchitis.
OLYMPAD TRAPS TO AVOID
  • Do NOT assume low serum T means primary testicular failure — always check LH/FSH
  • Do NOT assume azoospermia means hormonal failure — post-testicular obstruction has normal hormones
  • Do NOT confuse 5α-reductase deficiency (low DHT, normal T) with AIS (high T, defective AR)
  • Do NOT forget that sildenafil requires NO to work — it is not an aphrodisiac
  • Do NOT forget that FSH acts on Sertoli cells, not germ cells directly
  • Do NOT forget that cryptorchidism causes germ cell damage via TEMPERATURE, not just position
